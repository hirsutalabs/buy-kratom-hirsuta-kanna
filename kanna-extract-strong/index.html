History & Ethnobotany of Sceletium tortuosum

Archaeological evidence places Sceletium tortuosum use in southern Africa at least 2,000 years ago. The Khoikhoi traditionally fermented the plant — a process that transforms the alkaloid ratio, increasing mesembrine relative to other compounds. European explorers first documented Kanna use in the late 17th century. Today it is among the most pharmacologically studied southern African plants, with multiple peer-reviewed studies examining its mesembrine alkaloid group.

Alkaloid Profile

Sceletium tortuosum contains a unique alkaloid group: mesembrine, mesembrenone, mesembrenol and tortuosamine. Mesembrine is the primary active compound, with mesembrenone present in significant secondary concentration. The Strong Extract retains the natural alkaloid balance in concentrated form without isolating individual compounds — preserving the authentic phytochemical character of the plant.

FAQ

What is Kanna and why is it botanically significant?

Kanna (Sceletium tortuosum) is a small succulent from South Africa's Western Cape with a 2,000+ year history of ethnobotanical use. Its significance lies in its unique alkaloid group — particularly mesembrine and mesembrenone — which have attracted substantial pharmacological research interest and make it one of the few southern African plants with both documented indigenous use and modern scientific study.

What is the difference between Strong, Ultra Strong, Crystal, Full Spectrum and Happy Calming Kanna?

Strong Extract preserves the full alkaloid range in concentrated form. Ultra Strong undergoes additional concentration stages for higher alkaloid density. Crystal isolates specific alkaloids into crystalline form. Full Spectrum preserves the complete natural inter-alkaloid ratios. Happy Calming emphasises the calming alkaloid fraction. Strong Extract is the ideal starting point for collectors new to Kanna research.

How does extract powder differ from raw Sceletium tortuosum leaf?

Raw leaf contains alkaloids distributed throughout the plant matrix alongside cellulose, chlorophyll and waxes. The extraction process concentrates alkaloids by removing non-alkaloid material, resulting in a powder with significantly higher alkaloid density per gram than raw dried leaf — making it a more efficient research substrate.
